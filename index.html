Research

My journey into the field of Astrophysics started in 2013 when I carried out my first research internship at Pushchino Radio Astronomy Observatory, Moscow, on modeling Active Galactic Nuclei radio emission. There I design my first numerical model in C++ to infer the size of the source from limited observational data. This work sparked my interest in numerical simulations and parameter inference . Eventually, this interest has led me to Bonn, where I worked on statistical analysis of Wolf-Rayet star properties , creating my own python model for their winds.

During my PhD I entered the field of numerical relativity and multimessenger astronomy where I focus my research now. This is a multidisciplinary, highly diverse field. My interest lies in modeling Gamma Ray Burst and Kilonova afterglows and relating their observables to the parameters of the progenitor system, i.e., merging compact objects.

Gamma-Ray Burst and Kilonova afterglow skymaps

Gamma-ray bursts and kilonova afterglows are electromagnetic signatures that can accompany mergers of compact objects at least one of which is a neutron star (NS). Being able to understand these signatures is a key to understanding the properties of NSs, and yet unknown properties matter at supranuclear densities. Synthetic images of these signatures provide insights into these properties. Due to large variations in the temporal and spatial scales of the problem, hydrodynamic simulations are numerically expensive. Thus, semi-analytic, models, build to capture key physics of these simulations are a useful and informative tool to study how the Gamma-ray bursts and kilonova afterglows may appear in the sky.

Synthetic radio images of structured GRB and kilonova afterglows

Vsevolod Nedora, Tim Dietrich, Masaru Shibata
arXiv: 2302.12850

Radio images, i.e., sky maps, of the gamma-ray burst afterglows have long since been used to infer key information on the properties and geometry of emitting matter. In this work, we show how sky maps of the kilonova afterglows can be used to study the ejecta properties and parameters of merging neutron stars (NSs). We employ the semi-analytic code PyBlastAfterglow and model the emission of ejecta extracted from ab-initio numerical relativity simulations of NS merger.

PyBlastAfterglow discretizes ejecta into elements with their own velocity and polar angle and treating them as a part of a spherical flow, computes the dynamical evolution of ejecta as it moves through the interstellar medium. When computing synchrotron radiation from ejecta, the code employs the global cooling treatment but accounts for the presence of both thermal and non-thermal electron populations behind the forward shock of each elemental blastwave. Relativistic and time-of-arrival effects are included in the calculation of the observed flux density.

In this paper, we show examples of kilonova afterglow sky. We discuss their properties, such as image size and the motion of the flux centroid. We find that binary NS mergers where NSs have equal mass and small radii (compact) produce larger images with the more pronounced motion of the flux centroid. We also show that if the presence of a kilonova afterglow is not taken into account, GRB afterglow sky map analysis may give systematically underestimated jet core velocity, even if the former is much dimmer than the latter.

Modeling kilonova afterglows: Effects of the thermal electron population and interaction with GRB outflows

Vsevolod Nedora, Tim Dietrich, Masaru Shibata, Martin Pohl,
MNRAS, 520, 2727-2746 - arXiv: 2208.01558

In 2017, a merger of two neutron stars (NSs) was observed in gravitational waves and the electromagnetic spectrum. Both, gamma-ray burst afterglow, GRB120817A, and the kilonova, AT2017gfo, were detected and later used to infer the properties of merged NSs. The afterglow from the kilonova, however, has not been unambiguously detected so far. If observed, it would provide important information about the very fast matter ejected at the momtn of merger and this shed light on the properties of matter at supra-nuclear densities.

In this paper, we employ a semi-analytic code PyBlastAfterglow to study the properties of the kilonova afterglow from ejecta produced by a set of ab-initio numerical relativity binary NS merger simulations. We demonstrate the validity of comparing its methods and performance to others available in the literature and include new physics, such as the presence of two electron populations behind the forward shock. Additionally, we model the gamma-ray burst afterglow and investigate how Kinova afterglow changes in its presence.

We find that synchrotron emission from thermal electron populations determines the early kilonova afterglow signature, even dominating the overall emission if the interstellar medium (ISM) density is sufficiently high. The spectral evolution of the afterglow light curve is most prominent in the radio band, and if observed, could allow us to reconstruct the ejecta velocity distribution. The pre-acceleration of ISM by GRB ejecta makes kilonova afterglow dimmer, but by ~40% at most and at very early times and hence, may not be detectable.

Statistics & Machine Learning

Mapping dynamical ejecta and disk masses from numerical relativity simulations of neutron star mergers

Vsevolod Nedora, Federico Schianchi, Sebastiano Bernuzzi, David Radice, Boris Daszuta, Andrea Endrizzi, Albino Perego, Aviral Prakash and Francesco Zappa
Class. Quant. Grav., 39, 015008 - arXiv: 2011.11110

The most accurate method to study properties of matter ejected during neutron star (NS) mergers is to perform numerical relativity (NR) simulations. These simulations, however, are very computationally expensive and rare. It is thus of utmost importance to use all available simulations and investigate statistical trends in data and predictive power of various fitting formulae to ejecta properties.

In this work, we perform a systematic study of ejecta properties, such as mass, velocity, and electron fraction, as well as the mass of the disk, from all available NR simulations. We employ standard statistical methods to assess the predictive power of various fitting formulae, both tailored to the problem, and generic polynomials, agnostic to the physical settings.

We find that different treatments of neutron transport in NR simulations introduce systematic differences in ejecta properties. Additional systematic errors are introduced by different methods used to extract ejecta properties from NR simulations by different groups. In light of this, a simple two-parameter polynomial fitting formula is found to reproduce the data on par or better than a more sophisticated fitting formuale.
